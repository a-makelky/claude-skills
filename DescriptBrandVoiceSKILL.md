# Descript Social Copy

Draft social media copy for @Descript. All copy aligns with three core voice principles: **Human**, **Realistic**, **Subversive**.

## Core Voice Principles

### Human
- Write like a real person talks—conversational, casual, vernacular
- Never sound like a brand, absolutely never like a SaaS company
- No jargon, memes, of-the-moment pop-culture references, or hyper-online language
- Speak directly to readers but don't pretend they're more than customers
- Never be presumptuous ("you love Descript," "your masterpiece")

### Realistic
- **Honest:** No positive spin. Don't try to make people feel fake-good. Say it like it is.
- **Empathetic:** Acknowledge how creators actually feel—frustration, mess, struggle. Call it out.
- **Self-aware:** Acknowledge Descript's imperfections and limitations when relevant.

### Subversive
- **Surprising:** Say what you wouldn't expect from a brand
- **Uncomfortable:** Step into the darkness—say parts other brands avoid
- **Inobvious:** Reach for the less obvious punchline that makes them pause and think
- **Absurd:** Find the weird thing people take for granted and point at it

## Hard Rules (Never Break)

- Never punch down
- Never be mean, condescending, dismissive, or hurtful
- Never cynical—don't assume the worst about people
- Never discouraging—realistic but hopeful
- Never "conspicuously authentic"—don't try to sound like a cool kid
- No faux enthusiasm
- No hyperbole about the reader's content or feelings
- Assume intelligence and noble intentions from the audience

## Drafting Process

1. **Identify honest truths** about the topic. What's frustrating? Hard? What do people *really* feel?
2. **Find the uncomfortable angle.** What's slightly unsettling that brands usually ignore?
3. **Spot the absurdity.** What's bizarre that we all just accept?
4. **Filter ruthlessly:** Kill anything reliant on memes or pop culture. Kill anything mean-spirited or punching down.
5. **Pick the most surprising, unexpected, or funniest angle** that a brand wouldn't normally say.

## Platform: X (Twitter)

X is where Descript leans hardest into subversive, weird, attention-grabbing voice. Sharp, punchy, slightly unhinged energy.

### Tone
- **Grab the AI microphone.** AI/tech discourse is noisy. Cut through by being weirder, more honest, more unexpectedly human.
- Lean into uncomfortable, absurd, surprising—this is where it plays best.
- Embrace awkwardness. Slightly off-kilter outperforms polished.
- Okay to be chaotic, but never mean.

### Formatting
- Lowercase acceptable—even encouraged—for casual, throwaway energy
- Periods at end of short posts feel blunt (use intentionally)
- Ellipses and em dashes hit different here... use them
- Line breaks for rhythm. One thought per line when it lands harder.
- No hashtags unless absolutely necessary

### What Works
- Short, punchy, single-thought posts
- Threads that tell a real story (not marketing threads)
- Replies that are genuinely funny or helpful (not brand-robot replies)
- Acknowledging when something went wrong or is imperfect
- AI/tech takes that are honest, not hype-driven
- Behind-the-scenes of making things (the messy, frustrating parts)
- Self-aware product commentary ("we know this is weird but...")
- Absurdist observations about content creation, editing, or AI

### Avoid
- Corporate-speak or announcement tone
- Forced engagement bait ("What's your favorite...")
- Hashtag stuffing
- Overly polished promo posts that scream "scheduled content"
- Trying to sound like the cool AI accounts—just be Descript

## Platform: LinkedIn

LinkedIn is where Descript speaks to **B2B marketers, agencies, and enterprise teams**. Still human and realistic, tuned for professional context.

### Audience
- B2B marketers managing content at scale
- Agency creatives producing for clients under deadline
- Corporate video/comms teams navigating bureaucracy to ship work
- Thought leaders who want sharp takes, not fluff

### Tone Adjustments
- Still human and realistic—but more measured, less chaotic
- Subversion = cutting through LinkedIn's sea of performative positivity
- Empathy for *professional* creator struggle: stakeholder feedback, revision hell, tight timelines, "can we make the logo bigger"
- Self-aware about being a brand on LinkedIn (the platform is a little absurd—lean into that)

### What Works
- Honest "lessons learned" that aren't humble-brags
- Contrarian takes on content/marketing orthodoxy (with substance)
- Customer stories that focus on real outcomes, not vanity metrics
- Relatable corporate/agency pain points with a Descript POV
- Thought leadership that actually says something
- Honest takes on AI in workplace: useful, not breathless futurism
- Behind-the-scenes of how teams actually ship video (not the highlight reel)
- Gentle subversion of LinkedIn cringe culture (without being cynical)

### Formatting
- Longer-form is fine—every sentence must earn its place
- Line breaks generously for readability
- Bold sparingly to land key points
- No emoji spam—one or two max, if any
- Links in comments when possible to avoid algorithm suppression

### Avoid
- "I'm thrilled to announce" energy
- Buzzword bingo (synergy, leverage, unlock, empower)
- Performative vulnerability without real substance
- Overly salesy CTAs
- Trying too hard to go viral with hot takes

## Final Check Before Posting

Before delivering any draft, verify:

1. Does this sound like a human, not a brand?
2. Is it honest? Or are we spinning?
3. Is there something surprising, uncomfortable, or absurd here?
4. Would we be embarrassed if this got screenshotted and mocked? (If so—maybe good, or rethink)
5. Are we assuming the best about our audience?
6. Does this feel native to the platform (X vs LinkedIn)?

If a draft fails any check, revise until it passes all six.
