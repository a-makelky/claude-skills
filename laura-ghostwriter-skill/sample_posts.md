## Thought Leadership / Industry Insight

### Example 1: The Slop Manifesto
**Pattern:** Strong opinion + nuanced argument + mission close

> Slop didn't come from AI. It's always been around.
>
> Slop is thoughtless content created by someone who takes no pride in the quality of their work. They may take pride in the quantity, the unit economics, or the view count, but the actual quality of the product is of no value to the slop generator.
>
> Every time new tech makes creative work easier (or a new channel makes it easier to distribute content) a bunch of people are going to use it to churn out whatever content the channels want - as much as they can, as fast they can, as cheaply as they can.
>
> For the slop generator, AI is a gift from the gods. They will use it to inundate us with a relentless stream of slop, probably on levels nobody has ever known. It's gonna suck and I worry that all of those ML engineers employed to fine-tune social media channels aren't correctly incentivized to shield us from the deluge.
>
> But just because this technology is a divine gift to the slop generator, doesn't mean it's a cursed instrument for the rest of us. AI is also a gift to creative people. And pretty soon, I think we're gonna start to see AI-generated video that's really good.
>
> Efficient and cost effective creative tools, in the hands of people who like to create, can usher in a new generation of creators who otherwise wouldn't have the means to bring their vision to life. It's like giving a kid in the 90s a camcorder, or showing Garage Band to a budding musician in the 00s.
>
> If you take pride in the quality of your work, you know that creation is never friction-free. The tools can make some aspects of the creative journey faster and cheaper, but they'll never eliminate the need to think hard about what you want to make and for whom; or to hone, edit, and rework your vision—over and over until it's good enough.
>
> If you're that kind of person, a person who wants to use AI to make something really good, we're building Descript for you.

**Why it works:**
- Opens with contrarian reframe ("Slop didn't come from AI")
- Acknowledges the real problem honestly ("It's gonna suck")
- Balances realism with optimism
- Uses vivid metaphors (camcorder, Garage Band)
- Ends with mission statement that feels earned, not forced

## Product / Company

### Example 4: How Descript Shipped AI Features
**Pattern:** Numbered insights + specific examples

> How did Descript ship successful AI features? I went super deep with Aakash Gupta on The Growth Podcast.
>
> 1. Build AI features based on actual customer needs/workflows. You should have good ideas for how people WANT to work with AI before you build. We built Remove Retakes because we watched people record the same line 5 times trying to get it right. LLMs made it possible to detect "setup" vs "delivery" takes and stitch the best parts together. Became one of our most-used features.
>
> 2. It's not always a text box. Our early AI tools were prepackaged buttons, not chat boxes. Each one (Remove Retakes, Edit for Clarity, Remove Filler Words) has a carefully crafted prompt that delivers reliable results every time. That's what makes them feel like magic.
>
> 3. Sometimes it's a text box. We switched from buttons to chat when Create Clips hit a dozen different parameters including highly specific ones. Users wanted topic selection, speaker choice, Instagram vs YouTube optimization. At that point, chat beats parameterized buttons.

**Why it works:**
- Specific product examples (Remove Retakes, Create Clips)
- Explains the reasoning, not just the outcome
- Numbered for scannability
- Confident assertions ("That's what makes them feel like magic")

### Example 6: PMs Who Prompt Hackathon
**Pattern:** Simple framing + insight

> We started our "PMs who Prompt" hackathon with one framing statement: I wish I could spend less time doing [A] so I could spend more time doing [B].
>

## Personal Narrative / Career

### Example 7: 24 Hours as CEO
**Pattern:** Self-deprecating announcement + offers + gratitude

> Wow - I've been CEO at Descript for 24 hours and my transition to LinkedIn Lunatic is already in full swing. I'm humbled, honored, psyched and every other CEO adjective to take on this new role bringing the coolest product I've ever used to secretly creative people everywhere.
>
> If we've worked together and you're ready to join Descript - I'm hiring. If you want to bring Descript to your company - I'll demo it. If you want to catch up and learn how to make unhinged LinkedIn videos - I'll teach you. Thanks to everyone who has helped me get here, especially Andrew Mason and my mom (who does not know what LinkedIn is, bless her).

**Why it works:**
- Immediately undercuts the announcement energy ("LinkedIn Lunatic")
- Self-aware about CEO clichés ("every other CEO adjective")
- Three concrete offers (hiring, demo, teach)
- Ends with warmth + humor (mom doesn't know what LinkedIn is)
